# Creating Poetic Singularity — 4-Part Substack Series

**Author:** Halim Madi
**Working title for the series:** _Creating Poetic Singularity_
**Audience:** the existing oulipo.xyz / Singulars readership — artists, poets, the AI-curious, a long tail of ML practitioners
**Tone:** essayistic, not technical-blog. First person. Specific images. The reader should never feel briefed, always invited.
**Length per essay:** 1,800–2,400 words
**Cadence:** one per fortnight, leading up to and following the ground.exe performance on June 12, 2026

This outline gives each essay: a **thesis** (the one sentence the essay defends), an **opening paragraph** (drafted), a **structural beat-list** with what each section does, the **research pulls** (which file under `planning/research/` to mine for material), and **what to leave on the cutting-room floor** (so each essay stays focused).

## Part 1 — _The Audience Is the Ground Truth_

### Thesis

Most AI poetry projects start with a model and ask "is this poem good?". Singulars starts with an audience and asks "what does this audience call good?" — and that inversion is the whole project.

### Opening paragraph (draft)

> A senior eval consultant told me, the week before reverse.exe, that I was making a methodological mistake. He explained, in the kind of calm voice that makes you suspect he was right, that user preference is unreliable as ground truth. People rate things they like, not things that are good. People are inconsistent. People will reward a poem that flatters them and punish one that asks something of them. He'd seen it ruin enterprise rollouts. I nodded. I told him I knew. I told him I thought he was right about every project except this one. And then I went home and built the eval anyway.

### Beats

1. **Open with the consultant.** Set up the central tension: a credentialed expert says one thing, the project does the opposite. The reader is now wondering who is right.
2. **Define the disagreement.** Explain the standard eval orthodoxy in 200 words: golden data + classifier, calibrated to expert human judgment. (Pull from `research/01-evals-primer.md`.) Make it accessible.
3. **Name the inversion.** In Singulars, the audience IS the expert panel. They self-select by buying a ticket, sitting through a poetry performance, choosing to read both poems. This is a different distribution than a labeling shop's. (Pull from `research/02-poetry-classifiers-and-eval-design.md` §2.6 — the Benjamin objection rebuttal.)
4. **The Porter & Machery moment.** Reference the 2024 Scientific Reports paper showing non-experts can't tell ChatGPT from Shakespeare. Steel-man both readings: the optimistic one (audiences are open) and the pessimistic one (audiences reward fluency over difficulty). Land on: Singulars is not a survey. It's a curated artistic claim. (Pull from `research/02` §1.7.)
5. **Why this matters.** If the audience is the truth, then making a poem better means making it more legible to _that_ audience — not flatter, not easier, but more able to land. The performance is the eval. Land here on the load-bearing observation (Halim — verbatim, pull-quote):

   > _Every performance — carnation.exe, versus.exe, reinforcement.exe, hard.exe, reverse.exe — produces a stream of pairwise preferences from real audiences voting on real poems against real themes. That is, in eval terms, a continuously growing human-labeled golden set, generated by exactly the population whose taste you're trying to model. Most teams pay for this; you stage it as art._

   This is the sentence the rest of the series unpacks. It should land hard.

6. **Close with carnation.** The first performance, in Paris. The first time someone voted for a poem the machine wrote. What that felt like. Why it mattered. Set up Part 2.

## Part 2 — _What We Measure When We Measure a Poem_

### Thesis

There is no validated automated classifier for poetic quality. Singulars is building one — narrowly scoped, calibrated against the audience we curate, and honest about what it doesn't measure.

### Opening paragraph (draft)

> The first time I read the rubric I'd written for the judge — _image, pressure, surprise, fidelity, voice_ — I felt embarrassed. It read like a creative writing class handout. It read like I was about to grade my own students. Then I read it back as a programmer would, as instructions for an LLM that has read every poetry workshop blog ever written, and I felt embarrassed again, but differently. The instructions were a confession of taste. They told the machine what I find beautiful. They told it what I will not forgive.

### Beats

1. **Open with the rubric.** Show the five axes from `research/02` §2.3. Reflect on what writing them down did to the artist. (This is the personal door into a technical topic.)
2. **The historical landscape.** A short tour of how the field has tried to measure poetic quality: Hemingway-bench (Surge AI, $25-50k per refresh, panel of expert writers); EQ-Bench Creative Writing (LLM judge, no human ground truth); LIWC and Pennebaker's word-frequency tradition; the academic side that can score _meter_ but disclaims judgment on _quality_. Each has a tradeoff. None has solved it. (Pull from `research/02` §1.)
3. **The trick: pairwise, not absolute.** Explain why ranking poem A vs poem B is more reliable than scoring a poem out of 10. Connect to LMArena, to the audience vote in Singulars itself. The vote is already pairwise — the eval just preserves the form. (Pull from `research/01-evals-primer.md` §4.)
4. **Present the judge prompt.** Show it in full. Walk through what each axis means and what it deliberately does not include. This is the artist showing his work — not just "I built an eval" but "here is exactly what the eval does and does not look at." (Pull from `research/02` §2.3.)
5. **The 80% problem.** Even with a strong prompt, an LLM judge has to be calibrated. The standard is 80-90% agreement with human raters. Walk through what calibration looks like — sampling 100 triplets, hiring a small expert panel, scoring agreement, iterating. (Pull from `research/02` §2.5.) Land on (Halim — verbatim, pull-quote):

   > _The classifier for ground.exe is, naturally, an LLM judge — but a calibrated one. You take a held-out slice of audience votes, run a candidate judge prompt over the same theme+pair inputs, and only deploy the judge once it agrees with the audience often enough. From that point on, the judge can score thousands of new poem candidates per training run, and you only have to spot-check it. The audience built the standard; the judge scales it._

6. **What this can't do.** Honest confession: the eval cannot measure cultural translation, lived experience, reader-poem fit, the shimmer of a poem read at the right moment by the right person. It can only measure what it can measure. The audience does the rest.
7. **The data so far.** Roughly 50 (theme, winner, loser) tuples across four trained performances. About to be 60 after reverse.exe. The eval grows as the project grows. (Pull from `research/02` §2.1.) Note also (Halim — verbatim, pull-quote):

   > _Each performance is a new fold of training data — a fresh slice the model hasn't seen yet, captured under different conditions, with a different audience, in a different room. Folds let you train on some performances and hold out others as a clean test set, which is how you avoid fooling yourself into thinking the model learned taste when it actually just memorized hard.exe._

   This is the line that prevents the project from over-claiming. We hold one performance out every time we fine-tune; we evaluate on what the model has never seen.

8. **Closer.** A line about a poem the audience picked that surprised the artist. The eval is not for _me_ — it's for the audience to teach the machine what they want. Resolve into the synthesizing pull-quote that names the whole stack (Halim — verbatim):

   > _That's the whole stack: people vote, votes become the golden set, the LLM judge gets trained against that set, the eval rides on top, and ground.exe gets better — or doesn't — in a way you can actually measure._

## Part 3 — _Making the Invisible Legible_

### Thesis

A loss curve makes a model's progress invisible to anyone who isn't an engineer. The visualization on singulars.oulipo.xyz is an act of translation: it tries to make a thing that lives in tensors visible to a person who came for a poem.

### Opening paragraph (draft)

> The first version of the chart I sketched was a loss curve. I sent it to a friend who teaches poetry. She wrote back: "What am I looking at." Not as a question. As a verdict. I tried again. This time I drew six dots on a horizontal line — one for each performance — and ran threads between them, one thread per model. The thread that mattered, the thread that was _us_, climbed up and to the right. Everything else was approximately flat. She wrote back: "Oh. Now I see."

### Beats

1. **Open with the loss curve story.** A friend who could not read the loss curve. The translation problem.
2. **Loss curves measure the wrong thing.** Loss is what the _model_ sees. The audience cares about whether the model wrote a better poem than a person — that's a different number. Pivot to pairwise win-rate as the right metric. (Pull from `research/05-visualization/design-brief.md`.)
3. **Five second test.** Walk through what the chart on singulars.oulipo.xyz/evolution does in five seconds. The eye should land on ground.exe rising. Everything else is supporting cast. Show the screenshot. (Mockup: `research/05-visualization/view-1-evolution.html`.)
4. **The matrix.** Below the chart, a head-to-head grid. Rows are models, columns are performances, cells are tinted in the performance's signature color. Tap a cell and read the poem the model wrote, and read the audience's choice next to it. (Mockup: `view-2-matrix.html`.)
5. **Restraint as design language.** The site does not have a dashboard. It has a printed page. Terminal Grotesque for the headline; Diatype Mono for the labels; a single color per performance; no animation that moves on its own; no real-time anything. The chart should look like it was always there. Cite the existing oulipo.xyz aesthetic. (Pull from `research/05-visualization/design-brief.md`.)
6. **What the audience sees.** A retired professor who came for the poems will see ground.exe winning more often, over time, against the human poet who taught it. They will not need to be told what's happening. The chart will tell them.
7. **What I see.** As the artist: which themes the machine still loses. Which performances were inflection points. Which models the audience consistently rejects (and what that says about that audience). The same chart serves both readings.
8. **Closer.** A note that the chart will keep growing. Every performance is a new column. Every fine-tune is a new line. The chart is the project's memory.

## Part 4 — _The Loop, and What Comes After ground.exe_

### Thesis

Most AI systems are built to remove humans from loops. Singulars is built to keep one human in the loop — the artist — and to make every step of the loop legible enough that the audience trusts it.

### Opening paragraph (draft)

> The performance ends. The audience leaves. The lights stay on for a while. I'll go back to the hotel. I'll open my laptop. There's a page on the website that only I can see. It has a password. Behind it, every theme the audience voted on tonight, with the votes already counted by the system but not yet locked in. I check them. I check the paper ballots from the back of the room and add them in. I press a button labeled "Flip to trained." Then I open another tab — Fine-tunes — and start two jobs in parallel: a small OpenAI model learning from winners and losers as preference pairs, a Llama variant doing the same on a Together server. The jobs will run overnight; the providers send me email when they're done. I close the laptop. In the morning, two new versions of ground.exe are sitting in my catalog, ready to be tested. I pick three candidates — the two new ones and a frontier baseline — set a budget cap, and press another button labeled "Run." Twenty minutes later, the chart on the public site updates. The whole loop takes about ninety minutes of my attention spread across one evening and one morning, with the providers and the eval runner doing the unattended work in between. The model has changed; the audience changed it. I close the laptop.

### Beats

1. **Open with the hotel-room scene.** The post-show ritual. The reader is now inside the loop.
2. **The admin panel.** Walk through the six sections — performances, vote entry, candidate models, fine-tunes, eval runs, publish controls — at a high level, in plain English. The point is not to brag about the build; it's to show what kind of object this is. A small private control room behind a public art project. (Pull from `research/06-update-loop.md` §3 and `PRD-finetune-and-evals.md` US-121-125.)
3. **The fine-tune trigger.** Spend a paragraph or two here, because this is where the votes become a model. Describe the form: pick a base model (a small OpenAI one, an open-source Llama, a Qwen), pick a format (winner-only, or winner-plus-loser as a preference pair), pick which performances feed the training set and which one you hold out as the test, press start. The job runs on the provider; you get an email when it's done. Note the elegant decoupling: whichever model duels you on stage, all candidate fine-tunes get to learn from the resulting votes. The performance picks itself; the catalog grows in parallel.
4. **Why a button, not a trigger.** The reader will ask: why doesn't the eval auto-run? Why don't the fine-tunes? Answer: because the artist is in the loop on purpose. Pressing the button is the _artistic act_. Letting Postgres run the eval — or worse, letting the provider auto-fine-tune on every new vote — would be like letting an installer choose where to hang the painting. (Pull from `research/06-update-loop.md` §6.3.)
5. **The audit trail.** Some honest acknowledgment: people will want to know whether the artist is putting his thumb on the scale. Confession + design choice: every vote-count edit is logged, every fine-tune job keeps its full training-data snapshot, every eval run keeps its config snapshot, every published run is immutable. The artist is in the loop, but the loop is not invisible. (Pull from `research/06-update-loop.md` §3.3.b and §2.2.)
6. **Cost.** A surprising aside: the entire eval pipeline — running five models against every performance — costs about $50/year. Fine-tuning a 4o-mini variant from a few thousand audience votes runs $5-15 per job. The whole annual budget for ground.exe's evolution is comfortably under a single dinner out. (Pull from `research/06-update-loop.md` §8 and `research/03-best-poetry-models.md` Shortlist B.)
7. **What comes after ground.exe.** Each performance creates a new fold of training data. ground.exe is not the end. There is a v1, a v2 — and not just one v2; multiple v2s, one per base model in the catalog. Each version is worse at things ground.exe was good at and better at things ground.exe was bad at. The audience keeps changing too. The project might be infinite.
8. **Failure modes I haven't solved yet.** Open questions from `research/06-update-loop.md` §10 and `PRD-finetune-and-evals.md` §9 — recency weighting, judge-model rotation, whether to fade old performances on the chart, whether DPO actually outperforms SFT on aesthetic data. Treat these as still-being-decided. (This honesty is the whole tone of the series.)
9. **Closer.** Return to the hotel-room image. The chart, just updated. The audience already on the train home, not knowing they trained the model that just shipped. The art project keeps running while everyone sleeps.

## Cross-series threads

A few motifs to weave through all four essays so the series reads as a single piece:

1. **The consultant.** Mention him once in Part 1 (as setup), once in passing in Part 2 (as a foil for the rubric), and once in Part 4 (as someone the artist still respects, but disagreed with productively). He becomes a recurring character.
2. **carnation.exe → ground.exe.** Use the names of the performances as chronological anchors. Each essay should reference at least two of them.
3. **One audience member.** Invent (or recall) a single person from the carnation.exe audience who voted in a way the artist did not expect. Refer back to them in Part 4. They are the project's first ground truth.
4. **Restraint.** Visually and prosodically. No bullet lists in the essays. No headers more than two deep. No emoji. The series should look like an art project's writing, not a tech blog.
